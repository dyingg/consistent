## Design Context

### Users
Productivity nerds who care about tools the way they care about gear — it should feel premium, considered, and worth their attention. They use the app at their desk, often in focused work sessions, to stay locked onto what matters right now and see where they're heading. They notice bad design. They respect restraint.

### Brand Personality
**Composed, precise, and quietly confident.**

Consistent doesn't shout. It doesn't motivate through cheerfulness or gamification. It earns trust through clarity — showing you exactly what you need, when you need it, with zero friction. The interface should feel like a precision instrument: every element justified, every pixel deliberate. Think less "app" and more "tool made by people who care about tools."

### Aesthetic Direction
**Reference points:** Apple's spatial clarity and surface hierarchy. Vercel's dashboard — the way it treats information density with respect, using typography and spacing instead of decoration.

**Anti-references:** Anything that feels cheap, childish, gamified, or over-decorated. No reward animations, no emoji, no playful illustrations, no bright gradients. Nothing that would feel at home in an app marketed to teenagers.

**Theme:** Dark mode, but not the pure-black void currently in place. Vercel-style dark: layered surfaces with subtle depth (think `oklch(15% ...)` base, `oklch(18% ...)` cards), enough contrast between layers to create spatial hierarchy without borders everywhere. Light on decoration, heavy on typographic hierarchy and whitespace.

**Color philosophy:** Near-monochromatic with surgical accent use. The goal colors (task categorization) are the only place vibrancy lives — they should feel like controlled bursts of signal against a quiet backdrop. Neutral palette tinted toward a single cool hue for cohesion.

### Design Principles

1. **Clarity over decoration.** If an element doesn't help the user understand or act, remove it. No ornamental borders, no decorative icons, no filler content.

2. **Typography carries the hierarchy.** Size, weight, and spacing do the work that most apps delegate to color, icons, and containers. A well-set heading with generous whitespace above it is more powerful than a heading inside a card with an icon.

3. **Surfaces, not borders.** Create depth through background-color layering rather than visible borders. When a border is needed, it should be barely perceptible — a whisper, not a line.

4. **Motion as physics.** Animations should feel like objects with mass — smooth deceleration, no bounce, no elastic. Things slide into place and settle. Staggered reveals on page load, smooth transitions between states, but never motion for motion's sake.

5. **Density with breathing room.** Show meaningful information density (time blocks, progress, upcoming tasks) but give each piece room to be read. Tight internal spacing within groups, generous gaps between sections. The Vercel pattern: a lot of data that somehow feels spacious.
